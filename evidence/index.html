Can LLM Agents Care About the World?

Exocentric Homeostatic Deliberation — Research Evidence Memo

arXiv 2026 Under submission Simulation code available

What this paper proves

Proposition 1 — State Boundedness

The composite welfare vector stays in [0,1]n for all t ≥ 0 under convex-combination dynamics and projected endocentric updates.

Holds unconditionally; does not prevent boundary saturation under heavy-tailed increments.

Proposition 2 — Monotone Passive Degradation

Aggregate welfare V(xt) is non-increasing on idle intervals where no action is taken and no surveillance update arrives.

Holds only during genuine between-episode idle phases; an out-of-band Wext update can increase V.

Design Principle 1 — Governance-Transparent Attribution

Each utility component's contribution to any action ranking can be computed and reported independently, without access to internal operational variables.

Algebraic separability, not statistical independence; λ coefficients must be fixed by governance.

Proposition 4 — Robbins–Monro Consistency

The mean recalibration rule converges in mean square to the true interventional mean μ*(a) under i.i.d. zero-mean noise and standard step-size conditions.

Restricted to fixed action, i.i.d. noise, infinite visitation; does not cover non-stationary socio-technical environments.

Proposition 5 — Restricted EFE Non-Equivalence

EHD and single-step EFE can yield different action rankings under two explicit mechanisms: mean-vs-distribution scoring (Gaussian–Gaussian case) and governance penalty.

Restricted existence results under auxiliary assumptions; not a general policy-divergence theorem.

Abstract

Large language model (LLM) agents can perform multi-step reasoning, use external tools, retain memory, and adjust their plans over extended sequences of interaction. However, one important issue remains insufficiently specified: the basis on which such agents evaluate whether a situation is improving or worsening over time. This paper introduces exocentric homeostatic deliberation (EHD): a framework in which the agent's primary welfare is linked to an externally monitored, auditable world-state rather than to internal comfort variables. The hope term is defined over an interventional distribution under explicit structural-causal modelling assumptions. Pragmatic and epistemic value enter the scoring rule as independently calibrated additive terms. Governance is handled through hard feasibility constraints and a soft penalty term, both inspectable without access to internal operational variables.
